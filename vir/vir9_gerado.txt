
Like all seaside towns it was pervaded by the smell of fish. The toy shops
were full of shells, varnished, hard yet fragile. Even the inhabitants had a
shelly look - a frivolous look as if the real animal had been extracted on the
point of a pin and only the shell remained. The old men on the parade were
shells. Their gaiters, their riding breeches, their spy glasses seemed to make
them into toys. They could no more have been real sailors or real sportsmen
than the shells stuck onto the rims of photograph frames and looking-
glasses could have lain in the depths of the sea. The women too, with their
trousers and their little high heeled shoes and their raffia bags and their
pearl necklaces seemed shells of real women who go out in the morning to
buy household stores.

The sun beat down on the parade, making the shells glisten, and the old men

seemed to be in no hurry, as if they had all the time in the world to
stroll, to chat, to watch the world go by. And indeed, it was as if they
were all part of some great, slow-moving machine, a machine that ground
along steadily, without a care in the world. The women, too, seemed to
be part of this machine, their footsteps echoing the rhythm of the men's
canes, their eyes fixed on some distant point, their faces serene. It was
as if they were all caught up in some great, unending dream, a dream of
seaside towns and shells and slow, measured strolls. And yet, beneath
this dreamy surface, there was a sense of unease, a feeling that
something was not quite right, that the world was not quite as it
seemed. The wind off the sea carried whispers of a deeper reality, a
reality that lay just beneath the surface, waiting to be uncovered.
